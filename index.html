The Plunge into Web Development: My interest in web development initially came from my readings of the textbook "Hacking: The Art of Exploitation" in middle school. The book entranced me, giving me an overall view of networking, cryptology, and exploitation. It was at this point that I started to connect with other "hackers" via online chat rooms. These "confidants" gave me tips on ways to improve my hacking skills, which for a large part included learning technologies such as javascript, SQL, and other common web technologies. It was my learning of these technologies that allowed me to start building out websites for local clients, providing me with job opportunities and allowing me to realise another aspect of web development that I've come to love: design. It's been my goal now to not only provide for my clients a website that's safe from vulnerabilities, but a website that's both functional and beautiful.

The Plunge into Robotics: As a child I would build a variety of scale model machines from Erector Sets I had recieved from holiday gatherings and birthdays. These kits allowed me to understand the inner workings (at least to a minor extent) of common machines including bulldozers, cars, and construction cranes. One particularly defining moment in my pursuit of robotics was the first time I had recieved a Vex Robotics kit, allowing me to build, program, and control a mini robotic rover of my own. Two years later, a group of fellow robotics enthusiasts and I helped start the first FRC (FIRST Robotics Compeitition) & FTC (FIRST Tech Challenge) robotics clubs at my high school, obtaining a Rookie All-Star Award in our first year of the FRC competition. Since then I've done research in path planning for multi-robotic systems, robotic networking, and human-aware navigation.

The Plunge into Neuroscience: The first year of university I'd been dorm-matched with a neuroscience major who was aiming to become a neurosurgeon. He'd converse with me through the night, nearly every night, about the inner workings of the brain, and in return I gave him some insight into robotics and machine intelligence. It was through these conversations that I'd come to realise that, in order to build more intelligent machines, it's necessary I first learn about the source of intelligence of our own species and extrapolate from there. I've since assisted in research papers within the field of computational neuroscience for a neuroscience lab at my university, where we researched gamma & theta wave modulation between the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and the hippocampus, assisting the field in its understanding of long-term memory consolidation.

Publications

Search and Rescue Operations with Mesh Networked Robots

IEEE Ubiquitous Computing, Electronics and Mobile Communication Conference (UEMCON) 2018

Columbia University; New York City, NY

T. Brodeur, P. Regis, D. Feil-Seifer, S. Sengupta

Abstract: Efficient path planning and communication of multirobot systems in the case of a search and rescue operation is a critical issue facing robotics disaster relief efforts. Ensuring all the nodes of a specialized robotic search team are within range, while also covering as much area as possible to guarantee efficient response time, is the goal of this paper. We propose a specialized search-and-rescue model based on a mesh network topology of aerial and ground robots. The proposed model is based on several methods. First, each robot determines its position relative to other robots within the system, using RSSI. Packets are then communicated to other robots in the system detailing important information regarding robot system status, status of the mission, and identification number. The results demonstrate the ability to determine multi-robot navigation with RSSI, allowing low computation costs and increased search-andrescue time efficiency.

Directory Navigation with Robotic Assistance

IEEE Computing and Communication Workshop and Conference (CCWC) 2018

University of Nevada, Las Vegas; Las Vegas, NV

T. Brodeur, A. Cater, J. Chagas Vaz, P. Oh

Abstract: Malls are full of retail store and restaurant chains, allowing customers a vast array of destinations to explore. Often, these sites contain a directory of all stores located within the shopping center, allowing customers a top-down view of their current position, and their desired store location. In this paper, we present a customer centric approach to directory navigation using a service robot, where the robot is able to display the directory, and guide customers to needed destinations.
